TV review: Between Life and Death and The Fairy Jobmother

Richard's life support was about to be switched off. Then he moved his eyes…

  • Tweet this
  • Comments (…)
    • Lucy Mangan
    • The Guardian, Wednesday 14 July 2010

The neurointensive care unit at Addenbrooke's Hospital in Cambridge is where, as last night's documentary Between Life and Death (BBC1) put it, death is not an event, but a process that can be slowed, interrupted and occasionally – just occasionally – stopped in its tracks.

The programme followed three people in the aftermath of terrible accidents, and charted their recovery – or not – from catastrophic brain injuries. Richard Rudd was thrown 60 yards from his motorbike. After 30 days in the unit, his paralysis seemed total. His family prepared themselves for the machines to be turned off. On the 31st day, his doctor, Professor David Menon, who set up the unit, asked Richard to look to his left and he did. And to his right? His eyes turned unmistakeably there. It wasn't like in the films. The doctor looked sadder than before.

Nick Holt's film gently parsed the increasingly nuanced division between being and not being. It also bore witness to the new sufferings, the new forms of grief that arise in tandem with the expansion of this strange netherworld, as families struggled to comprehend the differences between degrees of consciousness, the importance of memory, and to decide what constituted a meaningful conversation, consent and, ultimately, existence.

Richard recovered enough to indicate that, despite his total paralysis and lack of speech, he wanted his treatment to continue. Samantha Clarke, an 18-year-old student who was in a car crash on her way to class, did not. Her father, in tears in the corridor, asserted that his lively, beautiful daughter wouldn't want to live "as a cabbage". There was, at first, some hope that she would recover but as the week wore on, her scans showed increasing devastation to her brain. Handprints and locks of hair were taken, kisses given and then she died. "You would rather have a little bit," her father said, five months later. "You would still have that body to hug and if she could still slightly know what you were doing, you would rather have that."

Five months after Beckii Siemen's accident put her in a coma, she had regained what the doctors called a minimally conscious state. She was living in a rehabilitation centre and her husband had given up work to help give her the 24-hour care she will need for the rest of her life. She gazed out of the window and very slowly, softly stroked her son's hair. Whether it was a kind of reflex action or a defining instinct was just one of the many questions the programme posed and made you pray to God you never have to answer.

If you hadn't seen Hayley Taylor presenting Benefit Busters last year, you might have thought The Fairy Jobmother (Channel 4) was – like most programmes that have clearly been reverse-engineered from the title are – a spoof. Employment expert and professional neckerchief-sporter Hayley looks and sounds like Victoria Wood played by Peter Kay (or possibly vice versa). Then there were the clients themselves – toothless skinhead Dean, who has been out of work for two years; and sweet, gauche 20-year-old Maxine, who doesn't know if they receive £261 in benefits per week or per month. Any casting agency would have been proud to field these two.

But spoof it was not. Hayley and her neckerchiefs are real and together they are going to help Dean and Maxine face their fear of coming off benefits (a justifiable anxiety given the temporary nature of the jobs on offer and the six weeks it takes to process a new claim) and bolster their self-confidence.

As with all additions to the growing genre of poverty porn, the concept is black and evil at its heart (you can practically hear the producers squealing, "Look at these yucky people! Do we believe they want to work or are they secretly scum? Let's find out!") and ignores any and all uncomfortable wider truths about unemployment and the so-called benefit culture.

But at least Hayley has the answer to all Dean's problems. She coaxes him to get in touch with his feelings. "You look like I've just told you you're off down the clap clinic for treatment!" she roars gently when he responds with insufficient enthusiasm to news of a job interview. Then she coaches him for it ("Eye contact! Get yer 'ead UP! I'll fill in your scars with me eye cream. It's like Polyfilla!") and the programme ends with him starting on Monday. A final caption says that six months later he is still there and loving it. I suspect he wouldn't dare be owt else.

    yoshka123

    Does anyone else not think that this programme was a set up?

    Geeb, I think you're missing the point. Dean didn't so much say that he wasn't going to work for the minimum wage, he said that he couldn't work for the minimum wage.     I've read the C4 website and they say they're £100 a week better off now. I've done some calculations: £5.80 an hour times 40 hours, minus NI and Income tax equals £197 a week. That's £64 a week less than they were getting in benefits. If they're £100 a week better off that means they must be still receiving £164 a week in benefits. Of course, it's Working Families Tax Credits instead of Jobseekers Allowance, but it's still benefits.

    It would have been impossible for them to take the job without this. As I said, they couldn't manage on what they had, they'd never have managed with a £64 a week cut. The situation we have here is that a minimum wage job cannot support people at even a frugal standard of living without being topped up with benefits. Even with benefits, it's only £361 a week, still not a lot for two adults and a child.

    Perhaps people would be more inclined to take a job if the wages were higher? If they didn't actually lose money by doing so? (If you're childless, the situation is worse as you don't get as much Working Tax Credit as you otherwise would and so are far more likely to lose money by taking a job.) Why should the government top up the wages of people instead of employers paying higher wages in the first place? In a sense, it's benefits paid to employers as it allows them to pay a lower wage.

  • DaveSwift

    14 Jul 2010, 5:31PM

    I unfortunately didn't get to watch last night’s Between Life and Death, but hope to catch it on iplayer.

    The subject matter is very close to my heart and I'd like to share my story

    Just over a year ago, my younger brother (22 at the time) attempted suicide by jumping 70 feet. He was airlifted to the nearest hospital, and we were informed and were there shortly after. We were told the technical term for his injuries were "mangled" he had suffered severe head trauma and a whole list of other internal injuries. There was severe swelling of the brain and throughout the next 8 hours he underwent three or four operations on his brain, while they also tried to stop all internal bleeding and secure his other injuries. I have four brothers and four sisters and as we waited in the tiny waiting room, catching snippets of information from the doctors when possible, the news got steadily worse.

    After the initial operations he was in a deep coma, and we were finally able to visit him. I would hope that no one reading this ever what someone they love looks like after a 70ft fall. The swelling around his face made him look inhuman and completely unrecognisable, the scaffolding holding his body together along with the tubes monitoring and feeding him, made him look like Frankenstein - a pieced together reworking of my little brother.

    At about midnight half of us went home to get some food and supplies for the night ahead, and let the younger ones get a few hours sleep. About 20 minutes after we got home, we got a call from my dad, who put us on speaker phone while the doctor came into deliver news. Two nurses were in the background crying, as the doctor calmly told us that he was brain dead. half of us tried to argue with him as the other half broke down in tears, eventually I asked him if there was a chance he would regain consciousness "there's no chance" the doctor replied.
    The doctor turned to my dad and said "I'm going to need to talk to you about the next steps, but I'll give you some time" To which my dad replied "there's no way we're ever turning off the machine".

    The few of us who went home slowly pulled ourselves together and went back to the hospital to be with the rest of the family and say goodbye to Aaron. When we got there we were told there was more news. When they pulled back his eyelids, beneath the swelling Aarons pupils had dilated which proved there was brain activity. More family came to the hospital and over the next 12 days we waited as Aaron underwent operation after operation. We became experts in the different drugs which were administered to him and how they effected his vitals, we would sit and talk to him, as we watched the little monitors which told us everything from his heart rate to his brain pressure. We clung on to every bit of good news regarding these ever changing numbers, as it was all we had. 12 days after the initial operation we were told he responded to instructions and therefore at least had some real cognitive ability.

    We all visited him every day, and 6 weeks after the operation my mum was visiting early and noticed him moving his lips. She grabbed the nearest nurse who inserted a valve into his tracheotomy. Sounding like Stephen Hawking he then loudly asked "Can we go now please?". This was the happiest day of my life.

    A year on and he has recovered fully mentally, and while he is still largely bed bound, he is out and about on the wheel chair every day, can use his left arm completely, has regained strength and weight, and is as happy within himself as he has ever been.
    The whole experience has given me an unbelievably profound respect for nurses. The levels of care they give and the sheer strength they have day after day is awe inspiring.

    I'm not sure why I shared all this.
